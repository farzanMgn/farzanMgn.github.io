This is my personal startup story.

Zerynth is an IoT company that helps manufacturers and enterprises connect their industrial machines to the cloud. But there was a challenge: while their IoT kits were powerful, they consumed a lot of energy. Each data transmission drained the battery, limiting how long devices could operate in the field and raising costs for customers.

At zerynth , I was a Data Scientist. I faced the challenge of working with massive streams of IoT data coming from industrial devices. My role was to make sense of this raw data: cleaning it, analyzing it, and uncovering hidden patterns that could guide smarter decisions.

Energy is the key challenge in any IoT system. When thousands of IoT kits are installed across factories, it’s not practical to access each one just to replace a drained battery.

What matters most is saving energy.

These kits continuously collect sensor data and send it to the cloud every minute; creating an enormous flow of information.

But all of it is useful? NO!

For instance, during machine warm-up phases, the data carries little actionable value.

That’s where I came in. I developed models to identify and filter out low-value data, reducing unnecessary transmissions and significantly improving the energy efficiency and lifespan of the devices.

The latent space plot illustrates the two distinct machine states; warm-up and active, emerging naturally from the model’s clustering process for one sample device.

The effectiveness of clustering varied across devices.

An expected challenge given their different ages and usage patterns.

Older machines generated far more idle data than newer ones, meaning the model’s impact wasn’t uniform. To measure this, I analyzed 19 devices and tracked how clustering reduced their data transmission rates over a week and a month.

The results showed a clear trend: by filtering out low-value transmissions, the model significantly lowered communication frequency, ranging from 6–7% reduction in some devices to as high as 37% in others.

What made my journey at the Orthopaedic Research Centre so meaningful was witnessing how innovation could restore hope to patients who had long been discouraged by the high cost of rehabilitation.

In 2020, there was only a single French-made CPM rehabilitation device (Kinetec 6080™) available in the entire city, priced at $8,000 — far beyond the reach of most patients. The Orthopaedic Research Centre began work on a project called Orthopedic Biometrics, an ambitious project to design a local, robust alternative at just $600.

I worked on this project as a Product Manager for the Lab of Computational Biomechanics and Orthopedics at the School of Computer Science and Engineering, Ferdowsi University of Mashhad, where I led the end-to-end design of the Android application used to control the CPM physiotherapy device.

After the research team successfully created the first prototype of the CPM device, the next challenge was to build an application that not only control the device, but also let clinicians track patients, prescribe treatments, and streamline the medical workflow. That became the mission I was assigned to lead.

In 2016, Telegram had become Iran’s #1 communication platform, with 40M active users; nearly half its global base at the time. When iTele, the first third-party iOS client with a suite of enhanced features, launched, it quickly rose to the top of the charts.

I joined the team a year later, collaborating with the Product Owner to ship features that kept users engaged and ensured iTele stayed ahead of expectations.

At its core, iTele’s business model was straightforward:

Revenue = Number of Active Users × Price per User

Keeping the price per user fixed, we needed to increase the number of active users to grow revenue. That goal shaped everything we did. But achieving it was not simple. We faced a constant challenge: balancing two priorities at once; delivering features quickly enough to keep users engaged, while also building the visibility needed to attract new users.

To grow the user base, we pushed forward on two tracks in parallel:

  1. Feature Development – releasing popular features quickly, based on user feedback and emerging trends.
  2. Marketing & Visibility – running campaigns and driving social buzz to make iTele widely recognized online..

These two tracks reinforced each other in a loop:

iTele Growth & Revenue Loop

Popular Features drove User Engagement, Engaged users attracted More Users, More users meant Higher Revenue, Higher revenue and visibility supported Marketing Campaigns, which then created demand for even more Popular Features. This loop became the engine behind iTele’s growth.

My role as a Product Development Assistant focused on connecting these two tracks:

Prioritizing and filtering features that had the highest potential impact, guided by user feedback and market research.

Supporting marketing campaigns that engaged users and amplified product visibility.

Both efforts worked in a loop: we applied changes, tested and released them, gathered user feedback, refined the product, and re-engaged users through campaigns. This cycle repeated continuously, creating a feedback-driven pipeline that built long-term trust and engagement.
